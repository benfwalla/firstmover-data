This FirstMover Open Data Project aims to democratize NYC rental data and resources to renters, analysts, and anyone curious about the NYC rental market. 

The project started with two people moving to New York City who were completely stressed out by the apartment search. Beyond high rents, the sheer volume neighborhoods, rent guides, scam watches felt overwhelming. We ended up building [FirstMover](https://firstmovernyc.com) for ourselves- an app that sends you push notifications the moment a new listing goes live. In order for the app to work, we began collecting public listing data from public rental sites.

As the data collected each month, we started analyzing it. It occurred to us that other people might find this useful too. Especially right now, in wake up skyrocketing rents and Mamdani's intentions with housing. We couldn't think of many places where you could just download a whole month of rental listing data for free and start exploring it yourself. So we figured we'd make that exist.

## Why give this away?

Here's the thing about FirstMover as a company: our entire value proposition is speed. We're selling the moment that a listing appears. Once our push notification fires and you've seen it, that specific data point is basically valueless to us as a business. But it's *not* valueless to the broader community. A month of accumulated listings is a rich dataset that analysts, journalists, researchers, and regular people trying to make sense of their rent can actually use.

So the logic was pretty simple. The data has already served its purpose for our app. Rather than letting it sit on a hard drive somewhere, we might as well give it to people who can do something with it.

## What we're building here

At the beginning of every month, we publish last month's rental listing data as a downloadable CSV on the [Open Data](/open-data) page. Every row is a listing as it first appeared on the market. 34 columns. No copyrighted content, no descriptions, just the structured facts.

We're also aggregating other datasets we come across. For example, the NYC Rent Guidelines Board publishes rent-stabilized building records, but they're buried in PDFs that are a pain to work with. We cleaned that up, added coordinates, and put it in a [Google Sheet](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1_yUjWl9Z1z6T_8oRqXscOU6KFV25ECYgVO69lORFyxI/edit?usp=drivesdk) anyone can access.

On the side, we've been building some things with the data ourselves. Interactive maps, monthly reports, a "guess the rent" game. We put those on the [Resources](/resources) page. They're fun, and we'd be curious to see what other people could build with the same underlying data.

The [Resources](/resources) page is also where we're trying to catalog useful things for NYC renters. There's a lot of good information scattered across Reddit and random corners of the internet, and our hope is to collect the most valuable stuff in one place over time.

## Who this is for

Honestly, we're still figuring that out. There are probably two camps: people who want to nerd out on rental data and find cool insights, and people who are actively trying to find an apartment and just want good tools and information. Those are different audiences with different needs, and it might be a little awkward at first as we figure out how to serve both. We're okay with that.

If you have ideas for resources, datasets, or tools that should exist here, reach out at [firstmovernyc@gmail.com](mailto:firstmovernyc@gmail.com). The entire website is also [open source on GitHub](https://github.com/benfwalla/firstmover-open-data-project), so if you have something to contribute, submit a PR. We're open to it.
