January was rough for landlords in some NYC neighborhoods. After months of trying to squeeze every dollar out of renters, reality finally set in.

Here are the 10 neighborhoods where median rent dropped the most in January 2026, and what it tells us about the market.

## The Big Drops

| Neighborhood | Jan Median | Dec Median | Drop | % Change |
|---------------|------------|------------|------|----------|
| Saint George | $1,775 | $3,785 | -$2,010 | -53% |
| Hudson Square | $6,950 | $7,995 | -$1,045 | -13% |
| Red Hook | $4,200 | $4,875 | -$675 | -14% |
| Lenox Hill | $3,898 | $4,450 | -$552 | -12% |
| Tribeca | $7,200 | $7,695 | -$495 | -6% |

## What Happened?

### Saint George: The Reality Check

Saint George's 53% drop is the standout story. A median drop from $3,785 to $1,775 doesn't happen by accident. This screams "we priced ourselves out of the market entirely."

Staten Island landlords got greedy during the remote work boom, thinking ferry commutes would become the new normal. Turns out, people still want to live where stuff actually happens.

### Hudson Square: Luxury Fatigue

Hudson Square dropping over $1,000 shows even high-end neighborhoods aren't immune. At $6,950, it's still expensive, but landlords finally realized that $8,000+ for a neighborhood that's mostly empty after 6 PM wasn't sustainable.

### Red Hook: Supply Meets Reality  

Red Hook's drop makes sense. New developments finished construction, flooding the market with units priced for "the next Williamsburg." Except Red Hook still feels isolated, and $4,875 for isolated is a tough sell.

## The Pattern

Looking at the data, the drops fall into three categories:

**Overpriced Outer Boroughs** (Saint George): Got too excited during the pandemic, reality check was inevitable.

**Luxury Fatigue** (Hudson Square, Tribeca): Even wealthy renters have limits, especially when offices are calling people back.

**New Supply** (Red Hook, some parts of LIC): Developers' projected rents met actual market demand.

## What It Means

These drops aren't random. They're corrections in neighborhoods where landlords pushed too hard, too fast.

> The interesting part isn't that rents dropped — it's that they took this long to drop. Some of these neighborhoods have been overpriced for months.

The bigger question: Is this the start of broader corrections, or just outliers finally getting realistic?

## For Renters

If you've been watching these neighborhoods, now might be your moment. Landlords who just dropped rent by $500-$2,000 are probably motivated to actually rent their units.

But don't expect these drops citywide. Manhattan core, north Brooklyn, and Queens hotspots are still holding firm.

## The Bottom Line

Landlords in 10 neighborhoods blinked first in January. Some drops were long overdue corrections (looking at you, Saint George). Others signal shifting priorities as remote work hype cools.

Either way, it's the first real crack in the "rents only go up" narrative we've been living with.

More drops coming? We'll know by March.
